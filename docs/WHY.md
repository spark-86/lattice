# Why Create The Lattice?

So if we backtrack a ways, into yonder youth, I taught myself how to program in TI-Basic back in the 1980s. From the age of eight I was painfully aware of the weight of data. I wasn't one of those fortunate ones to have a cassette backup. I knew once the power flipped everything I wrote died.

This felt inherently wrong from the beginning. Not for the sense of loss of my work, but why should the attention be so much on the process and not the data itself. And then I got to the NES days, and the battery saved games. We started in the right direction. We held the state locally. The game recreated itself from state. We kept this idea alive for a while, actually. All your optical disc based systems needed local storage.

Until **The Cloud** came along. No longer were you burded with keeping your state locally. You could just mirror your copy in The Cloud. But there was always a price, somewhere. You paid rent for that capability, and it's very much at their whim for them to keep that agreement.

And then something happend... the entire world started carrying super computers. Not just _slightly_ better implementations of before, we are talking the modern smart phone has the whole capability of the 90s era Internet. In one device. And yet we never stopped paying rent to the people that are now holding trivial amounts of data, relatively speaking.

This leads us to the argument of why still do that? What if we could just host our own data, and via participation mirrors? If we all offered just 10% of our devices capabilites we make a mesh network on an absolutely incomprehensible scale. For effectively _"free"_.

I say "free" because we all know there's a power cost, but like, 10% of a modern phone compaired to the massive amount of telemetry overhead from Big Tech acting as a hoarder of what should be your data anyways? I'll take just hanging on to my shit, thanks.

This is why I made **The Lattice**. I just wanted shit to stay put with basically no cost. It actually started as trying to find a way to store industrial electric motor certification long after I was gone. Turns out most important things are just snippets of data. Not monolithic blobs of data, but either small facts themselves or pointers to a larger, hashable blob. So like, if I give a few GB to the collective, even just for a while, I get to have what I want stay around forever.

And then there's pointers to the CID of IPFS blobs. Like if you want to anchor something bigger, just hold on to it for a bit, until it gets distributed, and you can move forward to reclaim that usage. By giving up that percentage of your device you are insuring that through most people's normal usage, you can just... not lose your data. Ever.
